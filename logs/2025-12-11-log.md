Summary:
Synapses are the vital junctions where neurons connect to transmit signals, serving as the fundamental "bridges" for all brain activity from basic sensations to advanced cognition. Coined by Charles Sherrington in 1897 from the Greek "synapsis" (clasp), they allow dynamic, modifiable communication rather than rigid wiring.
Structure: Presynaptic terminal (vesicles with neurotransmitters), synaptic cleft (20-40 nm gap), postsynaptic membrane (receptors and ion channels). Transmission process: Action potential arrives â†’ calcium influx â†’ vesicle fusion and neurotransmitter release â†’ diffusion across cleft â†’ receptor binding â†’ ion flow creating EPSPs (excitatory, e.g., glutamate) or IPSPs (inhibitory, e.g., GABA).
Types: Chemical (90% of synapses; slower but adaptable via neurotransmitters) vs. electrical (gap junctions for direct ion flow; faster, bidirectional, common in synchronized areas like the heart). Termination via enzymatic breakdown or reuptake.
Synaptic plasticity: Activity-dependent changes strengthening (LTP: repeated firing boosts receptors via NMDA/Hebbian rules) or weakening (LTD) connections, basis for learning/memory. STDP relies on spike timing; modulated by dopamine/acetylcholine.
History: Cajal's neuron doctrine (discrete cells); Loewi/Dale's chemical transmission (1921 frog experiment, 1936 Nobel); Golgi's staining; modern optogenetics. Health links: Epilepsy (overexcitation), Alzheimer's (synaptic loss), therapies targeting plasticity.
