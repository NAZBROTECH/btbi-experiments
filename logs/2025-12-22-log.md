Summary:
Synaptic transmission is the core process of neural communication, involving presynaptic neurotransmitter release (triggered by Ca2+ influx), diffusion across the cleft, and postsynaptic receptor activation (ionotropic for fast, metabotropic for modulatory effects). Key players: glutamate (excitatory via AMPA/NMDA), GABA (inhibitory via GABA_A/GABA_B), with vesicle cycling (exocytosis/endocytosis) and plasticity (LTP/LTD via receptor trafficking, second messengers). Dysfunctions underlie disorders like epilepsy (overexcitation), Alzheimer's (synaptic loss), schizophrenia (dopamine/glutamate imbalance).
Research focuses on precise targeting: modulating specific receptor subtypes, vesicle proteins (e.g., synaptotagmin, SNAREs), or astrocytic support for better therapeutics with fewer side effects. Advances in optogenetics/chemogenetics allow spatiotemporal control, revealing therapeutic potential for mood disorders, pain, neurodegeneration.
