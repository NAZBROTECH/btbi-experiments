Summary:
Neurons communicate through rapid electrical impulses called action potentials, the "spark of life" that enables everything from reflexes to complex thought. These all-or-nothing signals travel along axons at speeds up to 120 m/s, converting chemical gradients into electrical bursts.
Key phases: Resting potential (-70mV, maintained by Na+/K+ pumps); depolarization (stimulus opens Na+ channels, influx raises voltage to +30mV); repolarization (K+ efflux restores negativity); hyperpolarization (brief undershoot); refractory period prevents backward firing.
Role in synaptic transmission: Action potentials trigger neurotransmitter release at axon terminals, bridging to postsynaptic neurons. Types include sensory (input from environment) and motor (output to muscles), with interneurons handling integration. Historical nod to Galvani's frog experiments sparking bioelectricity studies.
Health implications: Disruptions cause epilepsy (hyperexcitability) or multiple sclerosis (demyelination slowing signals); therapies like anticonvulsants stabilize potentials.
